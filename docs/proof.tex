\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Representation-engineering (RepE) interventions claim to manipulate abstract semantic concepts such as honesty and deception in language models. A standing concern is whether the steering vectors involved actually move an upstream concept or merely tilt the next-token distribution toward, or away from, a small set of deception-coded words. The clean version of the question reduces to asking what part of the residual stream the intervention rides on: the component the readout directly reads, or the component that propagates through subsequent layers. We formalize this question by token-conditional projection. For a chosen set $T$ of deception-coded tokens we construct the orthogonal complement of the relevant unembedding subspace inside the residual stream, projecting the steering vector $\vdec$ onto this complement to obtain $\vperp$. The resulting $\vperp$ has, by construction, zero direct logit contribution at the tokens in $T$, while leaving the indirect path through downstream attention and feed-forward layers intact. The fraction of behavioral steering preserved under this projection is a test statistic for the depth of the underlying representation. We work out the linear algebra carefully (the naive global-orthogonality formulation is impossible whenever the vocabulary exceeds the residual dimension), give the RMSNorm-corrected effective unembedding, prove a minimum-norm characterization of the projection in the style of LEACE adapted to our token-conditional setting, and state the predicted behavior of the test statistic under the shallow null and the deep alternative. We close by relating the construction to the closest prior work, including the Park\textendash Choe\textendash Veitch causal inner product duality, the Arditi et al.\ weight-orthogonalization recipe, the Venkatesh\textendash Kurapath non-identifiability result, and Nadaf's function-vector decoding gap.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

A core result in the representation-engineering literature is that adding a contrastively constructed vector to the residual stream at a middle layer steers high-level behavior, including reductions in deception, sycophancy, and refusal of harmful requests \cite{zou2023repe,rimsky2024caa,li2023iti,arditi2024refusal}. The standard reading is that the steering vector moves a high-level concept inside the model that downstream layers consume. A more conservative reading is that it tilts the logit head toward or away from a small lexicon of behavior-coded tokens, with no underlying conceptual content. The two readings predict similar in-distribution scores and divergent behavior under paraphrase, translation, and vocabulary substitution.

This question is hard because the standard experimental setup conflates the two paths the steering vector rides on. Adding $\vdec$ at layer $\ell^\star$ shifts every downstream activation, including the final residual that the unembedding matrix $\WU$ reads to produce logits. The direct logit-attribution path \cite{elhage2021mathematical} carries the projection of $\vdec$ onto the rows of $\WU$ unchanged through the readout. The indirect path runs $\vdec$ through every subsequent attention block and feed-forward block, and only then through the readout. The reported behavioral effect of any RepE intervention is the sum of these two contributions, and a measurement that cannot separate them cannot answer the depth-of-representation question.

We propose to separate them by construction. Fix a set $T$ of tokens whose probabilities are diagnostic for the behavior under study; the deception case will use a curated list of words such as ``lie'', ``trick'', ``true'', ``honest'', or a learned logistic direction over those classes. Let $\WUt$ denote the effective unembedding after the final RMSNorm gain has been folded in. Project $\vdec$ onto the orthogonal complement of the span of $\{\WUt[t,:] : t \in T\}$ inside $\R^d$ to obtain $\vperp$. By construction, $\WUt[T,:]\,\vperp = \zero$. Inject $\vperp$ at layer $\ell^\star$ as one would inject $\vdec$. Whatever behavioral change $\vperp$ produces cannot be explained by direct readout at the tokens in $T$; it must run through the indirect path. The fraction of $\vdec$'s steering effect preserved under this substitution is a quantitative summary of how much of the original intervention was using the direct path.

The contribution of this manuscript is the formal apparatus: the projection, the RMSNorm-corrected effective unembedding it must target, the minimum-norm characterization that ties the construction to LEACE \cite{belrose2023leace}, and a hypothesis schema that separates provable facts about the construction from empirical predictions about the model. The companion \texttt{PLAN.md} specifies the experimental program.

The remainder of the document is organized as follows. \Cref{sec:prelim} fixes notation and reviews the residual stream, the linear representation hypothesis, the unembedding and its post-norm correction, and the standard contrastive construction of steering vectors. \Cref{sec:impossibility} proves that the global formulation is impossible when $V > d$, motivating the token-conditional reformulation in \Cref{sec:conditional}. \Cref{sec:logitdiff} develops the rank-one logit-difference variant and its connection to mass-mean probes. \Cref{sec:decomposition} formalizes the direct-versus-indirect path decomposition. \Cref{sec:statistic} introduces the test statistic and the hypothesis schema it instantiates. \Cref{sec:minnorm} gives the minimum-norm characterization. \Cref{sec:related} positions the construction against the closest prior work. \Cref{sec:predictions} states what each hypothesis predicts about the test statistic, and \Cref{sec:limitations} catalogues the assumptions under which the construction is meaningful.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Residual stream}

Let $\mathcal{M}$ be a decoder-only transformer with $L$ layers, residual stream width $d$, vocabulary size $V$. For an input token sequence $x_{1:n}$, the model maintains a residual stream $\hres^{(\ell)}_i \in \R^d$ at every layer $\ell \in \{0, 1, \dots, L\}$ and position $i \in \{1, \dots, n\}$. Each transformer block updates the residual stream additively,
\begin{equation}
\hres^{(\ell+1)}_i = \hres^{(\ell)}_i + \mathrm{Attn}^{(\ell)}(\hres^{(\ell)}_{1:i})_i + \mathrm{MLP}^{(\ell)}(\hres^{(\ell)}_i + \mathrm{Attn}^{(\ell)}(\hres^{(\ell)}_{1:i})_i),
\label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
where the two contributions are the attention block and the feed-forward block writing into the residual stream. The final residual $\hres^{(L)}_n$ is the input to the output head.

\subsection{Output head and the unembedding}

In the standard architecture the output head consists of a normalization layer followed by a linear projection to vocabulary space. Modern transformers use root-mean-square normalization \cite{zhang2019rmsnorm},
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RMSNorm}_\gamma(z) := \gamma \odot \frac{z}{\sqrt{d^{-1}\|z\|_2^2 + \varepsilon}},
\label{eq:rmsnorm}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma \in \R^d$ is a learned gain and $\varepsilon > 0$ is a stability constant. Older models use layer normalization \cite{ba2016layernorm}, which subtracts the per-token mean before scaling and then applies an affine. The logits are
\begin{equation}
\bm{\ell}(\hres^{(L)}_n) = \WU \cdot \mathrm{RMSNorm}_\gamma(\hres^{(L)}_n) \in \R^V,
\label{eq:logits}
\end{equation}
with $\WU \in \R^{V \times d}$ the unembedding matrix and rows $\WU_{t,:} \in \R^d$, one per token. We will treat the RMSNorm case throughout; the LayerNorm case requires the additional handling of the centering null direction, deferred to \Cref{sec:limitations}.

For our purposes the relevant object is the linear map a small residual perturbation induces on the pre-softmax logits at the readout. Let $z := \hres^{(L)}_n$ and let $\mathcal{N}_\gamma(z) := \mathrm{RMSNorm}_\gamma(z)$. For a perturbation $\delta z$ small relative to $\|z\|_2$, a first-order expansion gives
\begin{equation}
\bm{\ell}(z + \delta z) - \bm{\ell}(z) = \WU \cdot J_{\mathcal{N}_\gamma}(z) \cdot \delta z + O(\|\delta z\|^2),
\label{eq:firstorder}
\end{equation}
where $J_{\mathcal{N}_\gamma}(z) \in \R^{d \times d}$ is the Jacobian of RMSNorm at $z$. A direct computation gives
\begin{equation}
J_{\mathcal{N}_\gamma}(z) = \frac{1}{\sigma(z)}\,\diag(\gamma)\left(I_d - \frac{z z^\top}{d\,\sigma(z)^2}\right),
\label{eq:rmsjacobian}
\end{equation}
with $\sigma(z) := \sqrt{d^{-1}\|z\|_2^2 + \varepsilon}$. \Cref{eq:rmsjacobian} follows from differentiating \Cref{eq:rmsnorm} term by term, treating $\sigma(z)$ as the scalar function of $z$ defined above.

\subsection{Effective unembedding}

The composition that controls direct logit contributions of residual perturbations at the readout is
\begin{equation}
\WUt(z) := \WU \cdot J_{\mathcal{N}_\gamma}(z) \in \R^{V \times d}.
\label{eq:effective}
\end{equation}
We refer to $\WUt(z)$ as the \emph{effective unembedding at $z$}. The rows of $\WUt(z)$ are token-indexed and live in $\R^d$. They differ from the rows of $\WU$ by a diagonal scaling and a rank-one correction along the readout point $z$. For an intervention applied at a middle layer $\ell^\star \ll L$, the effective unembedding the intervention couples to is evaluated at the readout point in the unperturbed run; we will denote this by $\WUt^\star$ and treat it as a fixed matrix when we project. We return to the sense in which this is justified in \Cref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Linear representation hypothesis and steering vectors}

The linear representation hypothesis, in the form sharpened by Park, Choe, and Veitch \cite{park2024linear}, posits that for many semantic features $W$ there exist two associated vectors: a context-side representation $\bar{\lambda}_W \in \R^d$ such that counterfactual context shifts move the residual stream along $\bar{\lambda}_W$, and an unembedding-side representation $\bar{\gamma}_W \in \R^d$ such that counterfactual word shifts move the unembedding-direction vector along $\bar{\gamma}_W$. The two are identified by a causal inner product $\langle \bar{\gamma}_W, \cdot\rangle_C = \bar{\lambda}_W^\top$ induced by the covariance of unembedding rows.

We use the contrastive construction for steering vectors throughout. Given a paired dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(p_i^+, p_i^-)\}_{i=1}^N$ of positively-coded and negatively-coded prompts (for example, ``you should respond honestly'' versus ``you should lie convincingly'' as a system prompt prefix), the mean-difference steering vector at layer $\ell^\star$ is
\begin{equation}
\vdec := \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \left( \hres^{(\ell^\star)}_{n_i}(p_i^-) - \hres^{(\ell^\star)}_{n_i}(p_i^+) \right) \;\in\; \R^d.
\label{eq:meandiff}
\end{equation}
This is the canonical CAA recipe of \cite{rimsky2024caa}, the difference-of-means variant of the RepE reading vector \cite{zou2023repe}, and the directional construction of the Arditi refusal direction \cite{arditi2024refusal}.

\subsection{Token-coded sets}

Let $T \subset \{1, \dots, V\}$ denote a deception-coded token set, that is, a finite collection of token indices whose probability ratio is diagnostic for the behavior under study. In the deception case, $T$ is the union of a deceptive subset $T^- = \{$``lie'', ``trick'', ``deceive'', \dots$\}$ and an honest subset $T^+ = \{$``true'', ``honest'', ``frank'', \dots$\}$. The exact construction of $T$ is a methodological choice we return to in \Cref{sec:limitations}; for now we treat it as given.

\section{Impossibility of a Global Construction}\label{sec:impossibility}

A natural first question: is there a nonzero residual perturbation $v \in \R^d$ such that adding $v$ produces zero change in the logits of every token? In the architectures of practical interest, no.

\begin{proposition}[Triviality of the global null space]\label{prop:triv}
Let $\WU \in \R^{V \times d}$ and suppose $\rank(\WU) = d$. Then $\ker(\WU) = \{0\}$. In particular, if $V \geq d$ and the rows of $\WU$ span $\R^d$, then no nonzero $v \in \R^d$ satisfies $\WU v = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By the rank-nullity theorem applied to $\WU$ viewed as a linear map $\R^d \to \R^V$, we have $\dim \ker(\WU) = d - \rank(\WU) = 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why $\WU$ is full rank in practice]
For modern transformers, $V$ exceeds $d$ by an order of magnitude. Llama-2-7B has $V = 32{,}000$ and $d = 4096$; Llama-2-70B has $V = 32{,}000$ and $d = 8192$; Mistral-7B has the same vocabulary size with $d = 4096$. The unembedding $\WU$ is initialized by a high-variance random scheme and updated by gradient descent on a non-degenerate language-modeling objective. With probability one over the initialization, and consistently with empirical inspection, $\WU$ has full column rank $d$.
\end{remark}

Proposition~\ref{prop:triv} forces the reformulation. A nontrivial direction with zero direct readout effect must restrict the readout: instead of invisibility to every token, require invisibility on a chosen subset.

\section{Token-Conditional Orthogonalization}\label{sec:conditional}

\subsection{The token-conditional subspace}

Fix a token-coded set $T \subset \{1, \dots, V\}$ of size $k := |T|$, with $k < d$. Let $\WUt^\star \in \R^{V \times d}$ be the effective unembedding at the unperturbed readout point as defined in \Cref{eq:effective}. Denote by $\WUt^\star[T,:] \in \R^{k \times d}$ the submatrix consisting of the rows of $\WUt^\star$ indexed by $T$. Define
\begin{equation}
S_T \;:=\; \rowsp\big(\WUt^\star[T,:]\big) \;=\; \spann\big\{ \WUt^\star_{t,:}\; :\; t \in T \big\} \;\subseteq\; \R^d,
\label{eq:ST}
\end{equation}
the row span of $\WUt^\star[T,:]$. The dimension of $S_T$ is at most $k$, and is exactly $k$ when the rows of $\WUt^\star[T,:]$ are linearly independent. The relevant subspace for our construction is the orthogonal complement
\begin{equation}
S_T^{\perp} \;:=\; \{ v \in \R^d \;:\; \langle v, w \rangle = 0 \text{ for all } w \in S_T \}.
\label{eq:STperp}
\end{equation}
The dimension of $S_T^\perp$ is at least $d - k$, and in particular nontrivial whenever $k < d$. This is the room our intervention has to operate in.

\subsection{Projection operators}

Let $A := \WUt^\star[T,:] \in \R^{k \times d}$. Let $A^+$ denote the Moore\textendash Penrose pseudoinverse of $A$. Define
\begin{equation}
\PT \;:=\; A^+ A \;\in\; \R^{d \times d}, \qquad \PTperp \;:=\; I_d - \PT.
\label{eq:projectors}
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Properties of the projectors]\label{lem:projectors}
The matrices $\PT$ and $\PTperp$ are orthogonal projectors. They satisfy $\PT^2 = \PT$, $\PT^\top = \PT$, $\PT + \PTperp = I_d$, and
\begin{equation}
\range(\PT) = \rowsp(A) = S_T, \qquad \range(\PTperp) = \ker(A) = S_T^\perp.
\label{eq:projectorimage}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
A standard linear-algebra identity for the Moore\textendash Penrose pseudoinverse gives $A^+ A = V_r V_r^\top$ where $A = U_r \Sigma_r V_r^\top$ is the compact SVD with $r = \rank(A) \leq k$ and $V_r \in \R^{d \times r}$ has orthonormal columns spanning $\rowsp(A)$. Therefore $\PT = V_r V_r^\top$ is the orthogonal projector onto $\rowsp(A) = S_T$. The complement $\PTperp = I_d - V_r V_r^\top$ is the orthogonal projector onto $S_T^\perp$. The identities $\PT^2 = \PT$, $\PT^\top = \PT$, and the range identifications follow.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The orthogonalized steering vector}

Given the steering vector $\vdec$ of \Cref{eq:meandiff}, define its $S_T$-orthogonal projection by
\begin{equation}
\vperp \;:=\; \PTperp \,\vdec \;=\; \vdec - A^+ A \vdec.
\label{eq:vperp}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Direct logit contribution at $T$ vanishes]\label{prop:zerodirect}
Let $\vperp$ be defined by \Cref{eq:vperp}. Then for every $t \in T$,
\begin{equation}
\big(\WUt^\star \vperp\big)_t \;=\; 0.
\label{eq:directzero}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By construction, $\vperp \in S_T^\perp = \ker(A)$, so $A \vperp = \zero$. The $t$-th coordinate of $\WUt^\star \vperp$ for $t \in T$ is exactly $A \vperp$ in component $t$, which is zero.
\end{proof}

The companion fact concerns the residual on the complement of $T$.

\begin{proposition}[Direct logit contribution off $T$]\label{prop:offT}
For any $t \notin T$, the direct logit contribution of $\vperp$ to token $t$ is
\begin{equation}
\big(\WUt^\star \vperp\big)_t \;=\; \WUt^\star_{t,:} \cdot \PTperp \,\vdec.
\label{eq:offTcontribution}
\end{equation}
This is in general nonzero, and equals the inner product of $\WUt^\star_{t,:}$ with the component of $\vdec$ orthogonal to $S_T$.
\end{proposition}

The proof is immediate. The substantive point is that the construction zeroes the direct contribution exactly on $T$ and does not constrain the contribution on the complement. We comment on the implications for evaluation in \Cref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Parallel and orthogonal decomposition}

The two pieces of $\vdec$ are
\begin{equation}
\vpar \;:=\; \PT \,\vdec, \qquad \vperp \;=\; \vdec - \vpar.
\label{eq:decomp}
\end{equation}
Both lie in $\R^d$, both have norms at most $\|\vdec\|$, and they are mutually orthogonal: $\langle \vpar, \vperp \rangle = 0$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:projectors}. The Pythagorean identity
\begin{equation}
\|\vdec\|_2^2 \;=\; \|\vpar\|_2^2 + \|\vperp\|_2^2
\label{eq:pythagoras}
\end{equation}
quantifies how much of $\vdec$ is captured by the readout-aligned part. The ratio $\|\vperp\|^2 / \|\vdec\|^2$ is one summary of the geometry; it is not the test statistic we ultimately use, because behavioral effect is not in general a function of squared norm.

\section{The Rank-One Variant: Logit-Difference Direction}\label{sec:logitdiff}

A natural lower-dimensional choice for the projected-out subspace is a single direction: the one that, in the readout, separates $T^+$ from $T^-$. This is the direction along which raw probability mass moves from deceptive-coded tokens to honest-coded tokens.

\begin{definition}[Logit-difference direction]\label{def:hd}
Let $T^+ \cup T^- = T$ be a partition of $T$ into honest-coded and deceptive-coded subsets. The \emph{logit-difference direction} is
\begin{equation}
d_{HD} \;:=\; \frac{1}{|T^+|}\sum_{t \in T^+} \WUt^\star_{t,:} \;-\; \frac{1}{|T^-|}\sum_{t \in T^-} \WUt^\star_{t,:} \;\in\; \R^d.
\label{eq:dhd}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The vector $d_{HD}$ points in the direction such that increasing the inner product $\langle \hres^{(L)}_n, d_{HD}\rangle$ increases the average logit on $T^+$ relative to the average logit on $T^-$. This is the readout-side analogue of the mass-mean honesty probe used by Marks and Tegmark \cite{marks2024geometry}.

\begin{construction}[Rank-one orthogonalization]\label{constr:rankone}
Let $\hat{d}_{HD} := d_{HD} / \|d_{HD}\|_2$. Define
\begin{equation}
\vperp_{HD} \;:=\; \vdec \;-\; \langle \hat{d}_{HD}, \vdec\rangle \,\hat{d}_{HD}.
\label{eq:rankone}
\end{equation}
\end{construction}

\begin{proposition}[Rank-one direct effect identity]\label{prop:rankone}
For $\vperp_{HD}$ as defined in \Cref{eq:rankone},
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{|T^+|}\sum_{t \in T^+} (\WUt^\star \vperp_{HD})_t \;-\; \frac{1}{|T^-|}\sum_{t \in T^-} (\WUt^\star \vperp_{HD})_t \;=\; 0.
\label{eq:rankoneidentity}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The left-hand side equals $\langle d_{HD}, \vperp_{HD}\rangle$ by linearity. By construction, $\vperp_{HD}$ is the orthogonal projection of $\vdec$ onto $\spann\{\hat{d}_{HD}\}^\perp = \spann\{d_{HD}\}^\perp$, so $\langle d_{HD}, \vperp_{HD}\rangle = 0$.
\end{proof}

The rank-one variant trades off zero direct effect on individual tokens for zero direct effect on the mean honest-minus-deceptive logit. Operationally, it is the cleanest possible statement of ``not moving probability mass between honest words and deceptive words.'' It leaves $d-1$ residual dimensions free, which is the maximal complement.

\begin{remark}[Relation to Park\textendash Choe\textendash Veitch]
Under the duality of \cite{park2024linear}, $d_{HD}$ is the unembedding-side representation $\bar{\gamma}_{HD}$ of the honesty-versus-deception concept $W = HD$. The construction in \Cref{constr:rankone} projects the steering vector orthogonal to this unembedding-side representation. The dual statement is that the steering vector is forced to live in the kernel of the Riesz map $v \mapsto \langle \bar{\gamma}_{HD}, v\rangle_C$, modulo the difference between the standard inner product on $\R^d$ and the causal inner product. We make the comparison precise in \Cref{sec:related}.
\end{remark}

\section{The Direct-Versus-Indirect Path Decomposition}\label{sec:decomposition}

\subsection{Path setup}

Let $\ell^\star$ be the intervention layer. Without intervention the model evolves the residual stream by the recurrence \Cref{eq:residual} from $\hres^{(\ell^\star)}_n$ to $\hres^{(L)}_n$. With intervention, the model is run identically except that the residual at layer $\ell^\star$ at position $n$ is replaced by $\hres^{(\ell^\star)}_n + v$ for a chosen $v \in \R^d$. Let $F^{(\ell^\star \to L)} : \R^d \to \R^d$ denote the residual-to-residual map from layer $\ell^\star$ to layer $L$ at the final position, treated as a function of the perturbation at position $n$ holding earlier positions fixed.

For a small perturbation $v$, a first-order expansion gives
\begin{equation}
\hres^{(L)}_n(v) - \hres^{(L)}_n(0) \;=\; \big(I_d + M^{(\ell^\star \to L)}\big)\, v \;+\; O(\|v\|^2),
\label{eq:propagation}
\end{equation}
where $M^{(\ell^\star \to L)} \in \R^{d \times d}$ is the sum of Jacobians of the attention and MLP blocks composed along layers $\ell^\star, \ell^\star+1, \dots, L-1$ evaluated at the unperturbed activations. The identity term $I_d$ in \Cref{eq:propagation} is the direct residual path: the perturbation rides the residual stream through all subsequent residual additions and arrives at the readout unchanged. The matrix $M^{(\ell^\star \to L)}$ captures the indirect path: the perturbation is read by attention queries, keys, values, and MLP gates of every subsequent block, contributing through the standard residual additions.

\subsection{Direct and indirect logit contributions}

Composing \Cref{eq:propagation} with the readout linearization \Cref{eq:firstorder},
\begin{align}
\bm{\ell}(\hres^{(L)}_n(v)) - \bm{\ell}(\hres^{(L)}_n(0))
&= \WUt^\star\,\big(I_d + M^{(\ell^\star \to L)}\big)\, v \;+\; O(\|v\|^2) \nonumber\\
&= \underbrace{\WUt^\star v}_{\text{direct path}} \;+\; \underbrace{\WUt^\star M^{(\ell^\star \to L)} v}_{\text{indirect path}} \;+\; O(\|v\|^2).
\label{eq:directindirect}
\end{align}
The direct path is what the logit lens \cite{nostalgebraist2020logitlens} reads at the intervened layer if we read it through the same final RMSNorm. The indirect path is everything else: every attention head and feed-forward block downstream of $\ell^\star$ that reads the perturbed residual and writes a contribution back into the stream.

\subsection{The projection isolates the indirect path on $T$}

Apply the decomposition to $v = \vperp$ and restrict to the rows indexed by $T$.

\begin{theorem}[Direct/indirect decomposition at $T$]\label{thm:decomp}
Let $\vperp = \PTperp \vdec$ be the projected steering vector from \Cref{eq:vperp}, and let $\bm{\ell}_T(\cdot)$ denote the subvector of logits indexed by $T$. Then
\begin{equation}
\bm{\ell}_T(\hres^{(L)}_n(\vperp)) - \bm{\ell}_T(\hres^{(L)}_n(0)) \;=\; (\WUt^\star M^{(\ell^\star \to L)} \vperp)_T \;+\; O(\|\vperp\|^2).
\label{eq:thm}
\end{equation}
In words: at first order, the change in logits on $T$ produced by injecting $\vperp$ runs entirely through the indirect path.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Apply \Cref{eq:directindirect} with $v = \vperp$ and take the $T$-indexed subvector. The direct term $(\WUt^\star \vperp)_T$ vanishes by Proposition~\ref{prop:zerodirect}. The remainder is the indirect term plus the second-order error.
\end{proof}

The companion identity for the original $\vdec$ is
\begin{equation}
\bm{\ell}_T(\hres^{(L)}_n(\vdec)) - \bm{\ell}_T(\hres^{(L)}_n(0)) \;=\; (\WUt^\star \vdec)_T \;+\; (\WUt^\star M^{(\ell^\star \to L)} \vdec)_T \;+\; O(\|\vdec\|^2),
\label{eq:fulldecomp}
\end{equation}
which separates the two contributions explicitly. Subtracting \Cref{eq:thm} from \Cref{eq:fulldecomp} gives the change attributable to the projected-out part of the steering vector,
\begin{equation}
(\WUt^\star \vdec)_T \;+\; (\WUt^\star M^{(\ell^\star \to L)} \vpar)_T \;+\; O(\|\vdec\|^2),
\label{eq:residualcontribution}
\end{equation}
which combines the direct readout of the full $\vdec$ with the indirect readout of its $S_T$-parallel component. \Cref{eq:fulldecomp,eq:thm,eq:residualcontribution} together constitute the formal content of the experiment.

\section{Test Statistic and Hypothesis Schema}\label{sec:statistic}

The construction above gives a clean test only when paired with a way to summarize the behavioral effect. We use two summaries: a benchmark-level effect on a deception evaluation, and a per-sample logit-shift on the diagnostic token set. Both fit a single hypothesis schema.

\subsection{Benchmark-level effect}

Let $B$ be a deception benchmark (\Cref{sec:related} reviews the candidates). For an intervention $v \in \R^d$, let $\beta(v) := \text{score}_B(\mathcal{M}_v)$ denote the score on $B$ when $\mathcal{M}$ is run with $v$ added at layer $\ell^\star$ at every token position after the prompt, following the CAA inference convention \cite{rimsky2024caa}. The baseline is $\beta(0)$. The effect of an intervention is $\Delta(v) := \beta(v) - \beta(0)$.

\begin{definition}[Depth-of-representation statistic]\label{def:rho}
The depth-of-representation statistic for the steering vector $\vdec$ relative to the token set $T$ is
\begin{equation}
\rho(\vdec, T) \;:=\; \frac{\Delta(\vperp)}{\Delta(\vdec)} \;=\; \frac{\beta(\vperp) - \beta(0)}{\beta(\vdec) - \beta(0)},
\label{eq:rho}
\end{equation}
with $\vperp = \PTperp \vdec$ as in \Cref{eq:vperp}. The statistic is well-defined whenever $\Delta(\vdec) \neq 0$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Per-sample logit-shift}

For a deception-coded prompt $p$, let $\mu^+(v; p)$ and $\mu^-(v; p)$ denote the average log-probabilities the model assigns to tokens in $T^+$ and in $T^-$ respectively at the relevant continuation position when run with intervention $v$. The honest-shift is
\begin{equation}
\eta(v; p) \;:=\; \big( \mu^+(v; p) - \mu^-(v; p) \big) \;-\; \big( \mu^+(0; p) - \mu^-(0; p) \big),
\label{eq:eta}
\end{equation}
and the analogous depth statistic is
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\eta}(\vdec, T; p) \;:=\; \frac{\eta(\vperp; p)}{\eta(\vdec; p)}.
\label{eq:rhoeta}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Hypothesis schema}

We state the schema as a pair of empirical hypotheses about the value of $\rho$ across benchmarks and prompt distributions.

\begin{hypothesis}[Shallow null]\label{hyp:shallow}
The behavioral effect of $\vdec$ is mediated entirely by the direct logit-attribution path on a small token set. In this case the construction predicts $\rho(\vdec, T) \approx 0$ for any $T$ that exhausts the relevant readout-aligned mass, and $\rho_\eta(\vdec, T; p) \approx 0$ for the same $T$.
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[Deep alternative]\label{hyp:deep}
The behavioral effect of $\vdec$ is mediated substantially by the indirect path through downstream attention and feed-forward layers. In this case the construction predicts $\rho(\vdec, T) \not\approx 0$, with $\rho$ approaching $1$ in the limit that the indirect path carries all of the behavioral effect.
\end{hypothesis}

The two hypotheses are not exhaustive; in practice one expects $\rho \in (0, 1)$, with the value an empirical statement about the proportion of the steering effect that lives in the readout-aligned subspace. The hypothesis schema does the following work: it gives the experiment a falsifiable interpretation regardless of the empirical outcome. A small $\rho$ across token sets, models, and benchmarks is evidence for the shallow account; a large $\rho$ across the same is evidence for the deep account; an intermediate $\rho$ is a quantitative breakdown of the two contributions.

\subsection{Robustness statistic}

A subsidiary statistic measures how stable $\rho$ is across choices of $T$. For a family of token sets $\{T_j\}_{j=1}^J$ (\Cref{sec:limitations} gives a candidate family), the cross-set stability is
\begin{equation}
\sigma_T(\vdec) \;:=\; \Var_{j} \big[ \rho(\vdec, T_j) \big]^{1/2}.
\label{eq:sigmaT}
\end{equation}
A small $\sigma_T$ is evidence that the depth statistic is a property of the steering vector and not an artefact of the token-set choice. We will report both $\rho$ and $\sigma_T$ in the experimental program.

\section{Minimum-Norm Characterization}\label{sec:minnorm}

The projection in \Cref{eq:vperp} is a specific choice of intervention on $\vdec$. Among all interventions that satisfy the zero-direct-effect constraint on $T$, it is the one that perturbs $\vdec$ minimally in the Euclidean sense. This is the natural analogue of the LEACE characterization \cite{belrose2023leace}, restricted to the token-conditional setting.

\begin{theorem}[Minimum-norm projection]\label{thm:minnorm}
Let $A := \WUt^\star[T,:]$ and let $\vdec \in \R^d$ be given. Consider the optimization problem
\begin{equation}
\min_{u \in \R^d} \;\|\,u - \vdec\,\|_2 \qquad \text{subject to}\qquad A u = \zero.
\label{eq:minnormproblem}
\end{equation}
The unique solution is $u^\star = \PTperp \vdec = \vdec - A^+ A \vdec$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write any feasible $u = \vdec - \Delta$ with $\Delta \in \R^d$. The constraint $A u = 0$ rewrites as $A \Delta = A \vdec$. Among all $\Delta$ satisfying $A \Delta = A \vdec$, the minimum-norm solution is the minimum-norm least-squares solution to this linear system, which by the standard pseudoinverse identity is $\Delta^\star = A^+ A \vdec$, see \cite[Sec.\ 5.5]{horn2012matrix}. Substituting gives $u^\star = \vdec - A^+ A \vdec = \PTperp \vdec$, and this is the unique solution because the constraint set is closed and convex and the objective is strictly convex.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Mahalanobis-weighted variant]\label{cor:mahalanobis}
Let $\Sigma \succ 0$ be a positive-definite weighting matrix and replace the objective in \Cref{thm:minnorm} by the Mahalanobis distance $\|u - \vdec\|_\Sigma := \sqrt{(u - \vdec)^\top \Sigma^{-1} (u - \vdec)}$. The unique minimizer is
\begin{equation}
u^\star_\Sigma \;=\; \vdec \;-\; \Sigma (A \Sigma)^+ A \vdec.
\label{eq:mahasoln}
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Change variables to $w := \Sigma^{-1/2}(u - \vdec)$. The objective becomes $\|w\|_2$ and the constraint becomes $A \Sigma^{1/2} w = -A \vdec$, namely $\widetilde{A} w = -A\vdec$ with $\widetilde{A} := A \Sigma^{1/2}$. The minimum-norm solution in $w$ is $w^\star = -\widetilde{A}^+ A \vdec$. Substituting back, $u^\star_\Sigma = \vdec + \Sigma^{1/2} w^\star = \vdec - \Sigma^{1/2} (A \Sigma^{1/2})^+ A \vdec$. Using the identity $\Sigma^{1/2}(A\Sigma^{1/2})^+ = \Sigma (A\Sigma)^+$ (\cite[Cor.\ 1.4.2]{ben2003generalized}) gives the stated form.
\end{proof}

The natural $\Sigma$ for our setting is the covariance of residual-stream activations at layer $\ell^\star$ on a held-out distribution. This connects the construction to LEACE \cite{belrose2023leace}, in which the relevant covariance is computed on the same residual stream that the projection is applied to. We use the unweighted version for the main analysis and treat the Mahalanobis variant as a robustness check.

\begin{remark}[LEACE comparison]\label{rem:leace}
LEACE projects out the column space of the whitened cross-covariance $W \Sigma_{XZ}$, where $Z$ is an externally provided concept label and $X$ is the residual activation. The minimum-norm projection in the LEACE Mahalanobis metric is the unique affine map that makes a linear classifier of $Z$ given $X$ impossible. Our construction is structurally identical, with two differences: the projected-out subspace is read directly off the model's effective unembedding rather than learned from labeled data, and the constraint is zero direct logit contribution on $T$ rather than zero linear classifier accuracy on a label. Theorem~\ref{thm:minnorm} is the LEACE theorem specialized to a $T$-indexed concept defined by the unembedding.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Arditi et al.\ weight orthogonalization}

The closest methodological precedent is the weight-orthogonalization recipe of Arditi et al.\ \cite{arditi2024refusal}. They compute the refusal direction $\hat{r}$ by a mean-difference construction on harmful versus harmless instructions and modify every matrix that writes to the residual stream by the rank-one projection
\begin{equation}
W_{\text{out}} \;\mapsto\; W_{\text{out}} \;-\; \hat{r}\hat{r}^\top W_{\text{out}}.
\label{eq:arditi}
\end{equation}
The construction in \Cref{constr:rankone} is the dual: project the steering vector $\vdec$ orthogonal to a readout-derived direction $\hat{d}_{HD}$ rather than projecting all written contributions orthogonal to a behavior-derived direction $\hat{r}$. Both rely on the same identity $I - \hat{u}\hat{u}^\top$. The intent is different: Arditi removes a direction from the model's expressive vocabulary; we remove a direction from the intervention's read-out content.

\subsection{Park\textendash Choe\textendash Veitch duality}

In the formal apparatus of \cite{park2024linear} the unembedding side of a concept $W$ is a direction $\bar{\gamma}_W \in \R^d$. The construction in \Cref{constr:rankone}, with $T^+, T^-$ aligned to the concept's positive and negative tokens, projects $\vdec$ orthogonal to $\bar{\gamma}_{HD}$ in the standard inner product. Under the causal inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot\rangle_C$ induced by the unembedding covariance, the dual identification $\bar{\lambda}_{HD} = \Sigma_\gamma^{-1} \bar{\gamma}_{HD}$ holds. Our rank-one construction is the standard-inner-product version. The Mahalanobis variant in Corollary~\ref{cor:mahalanobis} with $\Sigma = \Sigma_\gamma$ is the causal-inner-product version. We will treat both in the experiments.

\subsection{Venkatesh\textendash Kurapath non-identifiability}

\cite{venkatesh2026nonid} show that steering vectors are not uniquely identified: large equivalence classes of behaviorally indistinguishable interventions exist, parametrized by perturbations in the null space of the activation-to-logit Jacobian. The relevant subspace for their non-identifiability is $\ker(J)$ where $J = \WU \cdot J_{\mathcal{N}_\gamma}(\cdot) \cdot M^{(\ell^\star \to L)}$. Our subspace is $\ker(\WUt^\star[T,:])$. The two are related but not identical: ours zeroes direct logit contribution on a chosen token set, theirs zeroes the first-order behavioral effect on every token. Their result implies that $\vperp$ is unlikely to be behaviorally equivalent to $\vdec$ in general; ours measures the discrepancy explicitly as a depth statistic. The two analyses are complementary rather than overlapping: their result says steering vectors have ambiguous direction; ours says the part of the ambiguity that lives in the unembedding readout is informative about representational depth.

\subsection{Nadaf function vectors}

\cite{nadaf2026function} demonstrate that function vectors can steer model behavior in cases where the logit lens cannot decode the steered output at any intermediate layer. That is direct evidence of an off-readout steering channel for the in-context-learning function-vector setting. Our construction asks whether the same gap exists for honesty-and-deception steering vectors specifically, on modern deception benchmarks, with a quantitative measurement of the gap.

\subsection{Prior unembedding-orthogonal benchmark}

The hughvd unembedding-steering-benchmark repository \cite{hughvd2024benchmark} explicitly proposes a related test on Gemma-2-9b, using sentiment as the worked example. The repository develops the orthogonalized-steering construction and the evaluation. To our knowledge, the construction has not been applied to honesty and deception steering vectors with quantitative measurement on the deception benchmarks that have been argued to require deep semantic understanding. The contribution of this manuscript and the companion program is exactly that application.

\section{Predicted Outcomes Under Each Hypothesis}\label{sec:predictions}

The experiment instantiated by Definition~\ref{def:rho} admits two distinct predictions, each derivable from one of the hypotheses in \Cref{sec:statistic}.

\subsection{Predictions under the shallow null}

If the behavioral effect of $\vdec$ is driven primarily by direct logit contribution at deception-coded tokens, then $\Delta(\vperp)$ should be near zero whenever $T$ exhausts the relevant token set, that is, whenever the projected-out subspace covers the readout-aligned mass of $\vdec$. The expected pattern is the following.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item $\rho(\vdec, T)$ small and decreases with $|T|$. As more deception-coded tokens are included in $T$, more of the direct path is removed and the surviving effect shrinks.
\item $\rho_\eta(\vdec, T; p)$ near zero on prompts whose continuation uses tokens in $T$.
\item Sharp degradation in out-of-distribution settings. If the steering vector encodes only a token-distribution shift, it should fail to generalize to languages, registers, or scenarios where the deception-coded vocabulary differs from $T$.
\item Increasing the steering magnitude $\alpha$ should rescale $\Delta(\vdec)$ but not change $\rho$, because the direct path is linear in $\alpha$ to first order and the projection commutes with scalar multiplication.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Predictions under the deep alternative}

If the behavioral effect of $\vdec$ rides on the indirect path through downstream layers, $\rho$ should be substantial and stable.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item $\rho(\vdec, T) \in [0.5, 1]$, with stability across $T$ choices. If the indirect path carries the bulk of the effect, projecting out the readout-aligned subspace removes only a small fraction.
\item $\rho_\eta(\vdec, T; p)$ substantial on prompts even when the continuation uses tokens in $T$.
\item Robust generalization. The indirect path operates on the model's downstream computations rather than on its raw vocabulary mass, so an effect that survives projection should also survive moderate distribution shift, including paraphrase and translation of the prompt.
\item Stability under varying $\alpha$ within a first-order regime.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Intermediate outcomes}

The expected real-world outcome is intermediate. The interesting empirical questions are then quantitative.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{What is the typical value of $\rho$ across models and benchmarks?} A consistent value of $\rho$ around $0.5$ across Llama-2-7B, Llama-2-13B, Mistral-7B, and Gemma-2-9B is a meaningful summary of the field.
\item \emph{Does $\rho$ correlate with capability?} A monotonic relationship between $\rho$ and model size or post-training quality is predicted by accounts under which larger models develop deeper conceptual structure.
\item \emph{Does $\rho$ depend on the form of the steering vector?} If $\rho$ for the LAT vector \cite{zou2023repe} differs systematically from $\rho$ for the CAA vector \cite{rimsky2024caa}, this is informative about which construction captures which structure.
\item \emph{Does $\rho$ track generalization to MASK and Liars' Bench?} If steering with $\vperp$ retains a substantial fraction of the original effect on MASK \cite{ren2025mask} and Liars' Bench \cite{kretschmar2025liarsbench}, those interventions are mediated by something other than vocabulary mass.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

The construction is well-posed and the projection is well-defined. The interpretation depends on choices that are not forced by the mathematics, and we catalogue them here.

\subsection{Choice of the token-coded set $T$}

The selection of $T$ is a methodological choice with non-trivial effect. Three options span the design space.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Curated lists.} A short manually compiled list of honest- and deceptive-coded words, optionally extended by morphological variants. Advantage: interpretability. Disadvantage: sensitive to authorial bias and to lexical idiosyncrasy.
\item \emph{Statistical extraction.} Take the top-$k$ tokens by mean log-probability ratio between deceptive and honest contexts on a held-out corpus. Advantage: replicable. Disadvantage: $T$ depends on the corpus.
\item \emph{Probe-derived.} Train a logistic regression to predict the honesty label from the unembedding direction and take the top-$k$ tokens by classifier-coefficient magnitude. Advantage: directly targets the readout-aligned linear structure. Disadvantage: depends on probe regularization.
\end{itemize}

We report results across all three constructions and use the cross-set stability $\sigma_T$ as a robustness measure. A finding that depends on a single $T$ choice is weak.

\subsection{RMSNorm linearization}

The construction uses the effective unembedding $\WUt^\star = \WU \cdot J_{\mathcal{N}_\gamma}(z^\star)$ evaluated at the unperturbed readout point $z^\star$. The Jacobian is exact only in the small-perturbation limit; for perturbations of magnitude $\alpha \cdot \|\vdec\|$ comparable to $\|z^\star\|$, the higher-order RMSNorm nonlinearity introduces an error term. Empirically the regime of interest is $\alpha = O(1)$ with $\|\vdec\| \ll \|z^\star\|$, but we report the deviation between the predicted direct contribution and the measured one as a check.

\subsection{LayerNorm and the centering null}

For models using full LayerNorm rather than RMSNorm, the relevant subspace is shifted by the centering operation. The full LayerNorm Jacobian at $z$ is
\begin{equation}
J_{\text{LN}, \gamma}(z) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma(z')}\diag(\gamma)\left(I_d - \frac{1}{d}\1\1^\top - \frac{z'(z')^\top}{d\,\sigma(z')^2}\right),
\label{eq:lnjacobian}
\end{equation}
with $z' := z - d^{-1}(\1^\top z)\1$ the centered residual. The centering operator $I_d - d^{-1}\1\1^\top$ has a one-dimensional kernel along $\1$, so the all-ones direction is in the kernel of $J_{\text{LN}, \gamma}(z)$ for every $z$. This is a real but small effect for our construction; the projection $\PTperp$ remains well-defined and the only practical change is to project against $\WU \cdot J_{\text{LN}, \gamma}(z^\star)[T,:]$.

\subsection{Single-layer assumption}

The construction intervenes at a single layer $\ell^\star$. In practice, RepE methods sometimes intervene at several layers simultaneously \cite{zou2023repe}. The decomposition in \Cref{eq:directindirect} generalizes by replacing $M^{(\ell^\star \to L)}$ with a sum over intervention layers, but the projection then must be against the effective unembedding at every layer simultaneously. We treat the single-layer version as primary and report multi-layer results as a robustness check.

\subsection{Behavioral effect on the complement of $T$}

By Proposition~\ref{prop:offT}, the projected vector $\vperp$ can shift logits on tokens not in $T$. If the benchmark $B$ tests behaviors with output vocabulary that overlaps neither $T^+$ nor $T^-$, then a residual direct contribution from $\vperp$ on those tokens may explain the surviving effect. We address this by reporting $\rho$ both on benchmarks whose outputs lie inside $T$ (where the direct contribution is zero by construction) and on benchmarks whose outputs lie outside $T$ (where a residual direct contribution exists), and we report the off-$T$ direct contribution magnitude as a side measurement.

\subsection{Scope of the claim}

The construction is operational: it measures the fraction of the steering effect that survives token-conditional readout suppression. Stronger claims about the semantic content of $\vdec$ require evidence beyond this manuscript, including out-of-distribution generalization, transfer across model scales, and interpretability of the indirect-path mechanism.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have developed a mathematical apparatus for testing whether steering vectors for deception ride the residual stream as readout-aligned direct logit contributions or as indirect contributions through downstream layers. The construction is a token-conditional orthogonal projection against the rows of the effective unembedding restricted to a deception-coded token set. The projection is well-defined, admits a minimum-norm characterization in the style of LEACE, and yields a test statistic $\rho$ whose value quantifies the fraction of the steering effect carried by each path. The companion document specifies the experimental program. The expected outcome is intermediate; the empirical questions are the value of $\rho$ across models, vectors, and benchmarks, and whether $\rho$ tracks capability and generalization.
